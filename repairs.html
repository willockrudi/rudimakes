Ampeg SVT Classic — Screen Resistor Failure & Full Retube

2026-03-30 • Fixed

amp ampeg svt tube bias screen-resistor 6550 high-voltage

Device: Ampeg SVT Classic Bass Head

Symptom: Screen resistor failures — likely triggered by a shorted or failing output tube. Burnt smell, possible loss of output. Some resistors visually burned or cracked, others stressed but not yet failed.

Diagnosis: Visual inspection confirmed burned and out-of-spec screen resistors on the main PCB near the output tube sockets. The SVT Classic runs six 6550 output tubes at B+ voltages in excess of 500V DC — screen resistor failure is one of the most common service issues on this platform. Best practice is to replace the full set rather than individual failed units, as surviving resistors under the same stress will fail shortly after. Sockets and associated PCB traces inspected for heat damage and corrosion.

Fix: Replaced all 12 screen resistors with 470 5W wirewound units (one per tube position), ensuring physical clearance from the board surface for heat dissipation. Installed a matched set of six Sovtek 6550WXT+ output tubes. Powered up slowly, allowed a 15-minute warm-up, then set bias using a bias probe at each tube socket. Target: 28-32mA per tube idle current. Confirmed all six tubes within a few milliamps of each other given the matched set. Re-verified bias after 10 minutes under load — stable throughout. Reconnected an 8-ohm speaker cab, applied input signal, confirmed clean output across full range with no hum, noise, or oscillation.

Always replace screen resistors as a full set — the ones that haven't failed yet are already stressed. The SVT Classic is hard on output tubes. Keeping bias conservatively set and using quality matched tubes extends service intervals significantly. If screen resistor failures are recurring, check for underlying issues: marginal B+ voltage, loose sockets, or early tube failure. Recommend retube and rebias every 1,000-2,000 hours of heavy use. Documented final bias readings per tube position for service history.

Avalon U5 — Total Positive Rail Failure (Transformer Winding Open)

2026-03-30 • Fixed

preamp di avalon power-supply transformer rails diagnostics

Device: Avalon Design U5 Pure Class A DI / Preamp

Symptom: No audio. Voltage measurements on intake confirmed severely abnormal supply rails — positive side completely dead, negative side partially degraded.

Diagnosis: Filter cap survey taken first. C1 at expected -54V (negative rail alive). C2 at only -27V — exactly half expected, indicating half-wave rectification on that leg. C3 and C4, which should hold the +54V raw positive rail, reading essentially zero. Positive supply completely dead. All four rectifier diodes tested in-circuit — all showed correct forward voltage. Diodes were good. Replaced as a precaution regardless, powered back up — no change. Fault definitively upstream of the rectifier. AC measurements moved to the T5 toroidal transformer secondary terminals directly. Confirmed: one secondary winding of the T5 open circuit. No AC output on the positive winding. Single root cause for all observed symptoms.

Fix: Removed and replaced the T5 toroidal transformer with correct replacement unit. Reconnected all secondary wiring with polarity confirmed on all winding taps. Post-repair verification: all four filter caps restored to correct voltages. All regulated rails confirmed within spec. Unit returned to service.

Key lesson: follow the AC before chasing the DC. In any power supply fault, confirm AC is present at the rectifier inputs before replacing DC-side components. Every anomalous reading in this unit was a downstream symptom of one dead transformer winding. No regulator components needed replacement.

Fender Twin Reverb — No Reverb (Dead Recovery Tube)

2026-03-30 • Fixed

amp fender twin-reverb reverb tube 12ax7 signal-path

Device: Fender Twin Reverb Guitar Amplifier

Symptom: Reverb completely absent. Not weak — fully gone. Reverb knob from 1 to 10 produced no change in the dry signal whatsoever. Amplifier otherwise sounded normal: loud, clean, full output.

Diagnosis: First suspect was the reverb tank — easiest and most common failure point. Opened the tank and found a broken wire on the transducer inside. Resoldered the wire carefully. Reinstalled tank, reconnected RCA cables. No change — reverb still absent. Swapped the RCA cables and cleaned the jacks. No change. With the tank and cables ruled out, fault moved inside the amp. The Twin Reverb reverb circuit uses two 12AX7 tubes — one to drive signal into the tank, one to recover the signal returning from the tank. If the recovery tube fails, the tank can be working perfectly and nothing comes back. Swapped the reverb recovery 12AX7 with a known good tube.

Fix: Replaced the reverb recovery 12AX7 tube. Reverb restored immediately — full splash and decay returned on power-up. Amp verified at multiple reverb settings.

Classic example of a repair that took an extra step. The broken wire in the tank was a real find and looked convincing as a root cause — but the actual fault was the recovery tube downstream. Follow the signal path all the way through before closing a fault.

Leslie Speaker — Motor Service (Dry Bearings & Glazed Drive Wheel)

2026-03-30 • Fixed

leslie speaker motor mechanical lubrication service

Device: Leslie Rotary Speaker Cabinet

Symptom: Scraping, chirping, or rattling noise from the cabinet during operation, especially at slow rotor speed. Noise worsened over time.

Diagnosis: Removed back panel and inspected the rotor assembly. Two faults identified: dry motor bearings and a glazed rubber drive wheel that was slipping on the rotor pulley instead of gripping it.

Fix: Removed drive motor, cleaned old degraded oil from shaft and housing, added fresh light machine oil to bearings and felt wicks. Lightly sanded the glazed rubber drive wheel to restore grip. Reinstalled motor, realigned drive wheel to pulley. Powered on and tested at both slow and fast rotor speeds. No noise. Smooth Leslie swirl at both speeds.

Leslie motors last decades but they need oil. The rubber drive wheel glazing is a common failure mode on older units. A glazed wheel looks fine but slips under load. Light sanding is all it takes. Do not use solvents on the rubber.

Minimoog Model D — Oscillator Tuning & Calibration

2026-03-30 • Fixed

synth moog minimoog oscillator tuning calibration analog

Device: Moog Minimoog Model D Synthesizer

Symptom: Oscillators drifting off pitch during play. Notes sliding flat shortly after power-on. Tuning unstable — chords sour, oscillators not tracking each other correctly across the keyboard range.

Diagnosis: Classic cold-start drift. The Model D's analog oscillator circuitry has temperature-dependent characteristics. The synth must reach stable thermal equilibrium before tuning can hold. The unit had not been given adequate warmup time. The oscillator trimpots also required iterative adjustment to bring all three oscillators into correct tracking across the keyboard.

Fix: Full 20-minute warmup performed. Global Reset executed. Tuned OSC 1 RANGE trimpot against the highest A key using the built-in A-440 reference tone, then OSC 1 SCALE trimpot against the lowest A key. Iterated between high and low A until both ends tracked correctly. Brought OSC 2 and OSC 3 up in sequence, tuned against OSC 1. All three oscillators confirmed tracking across the full keyboard range. Synth left to play — stable.

The warmup period is not optional on the Model D. Tuning a cold Model D is wasted time. The RANGE and SCALE trimpots interact — expect multiple passes. Only a qualified technician should adjust internal trimpots.

Rhodes Electric Piano — Full Tuning & Pickup Setup

2026-03-30 • Fixed

rhodes piano tuning pickup voicing electric-piano setup

Device: Rhodes Electric Piano (Stage)

Symptom: Multiple notes out of tune across the keyboard. Uneven output volume across registers. Tone lacking characteristic Rhodes bark on hard keystrikes.

Diagnosis: Checked all 88 notes against a tuner. Identified flat and sharp notes and notes with beat oscillation. Pickup heights visually inconsistent across registers.

Fix: Tuning: adjusted tine springs note by note across all 88 keys. Moving a spring toward the tip raises pitch, toward the base lowers pitch. Pickup voicing: adjusted pickup height on all notes needing correction. Target: clean bell tone at soft velocity, characteristic bark beginning at medium-hard velocity, tine clearing the pickup face at maximum strike. Final check: full chords across all registers, tuning even, volume balanced, tone consistent from bass to treble.

Rhodes setup is iterative — tuning affects voicing and pickup positioning can subtly affect perceived pitch at high velocities. Work systematically from bass to treble. The right pickup distance is where soft equals warm bell and hard equals bark. Trust your ears.

Korg Triton — Factory Reset & Sound Reload

2026-03-30 • Fixed

synth korg triton workstation factory-reset firmware floppy

Device: Korg Triton Music Workstation

Symptom: Sounds corrupted or missing. Required return to factory default state with all original programs, combinations, and samples restored.

Diagnosis: The Triton factory reset is a two-part procedure. Step one initializes memory and wipes all sounds. Step two reloads the original factory sounds from the PRELOAD.PCG file via floppy. If the floppy drive is not operational before step one, the unit ends up with a single default sound and no way to reload. Verified floppy drive operation first before proceeding.

Fix: Memory initialization performed by holding ENTER + 0 during power-on. Factory sound reload via preload floppy disk using Disk mode. Factory programs, combinations, and drum kits loaded successfully. All factory banks verified and spot-checked.

The Korg factory preload files are available as a free download from korg.com. A cheap USB floppy drive works fine for writing files to 3.5" disk. Do not perform memory initialization until the floppy drive is confirmed operational. Keep the preload disk stored somewhere obvious — taped inside the lid is common practice.
